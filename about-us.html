About Us

Learn more about the Evaluation Hub project.

What is this project?

This project is about continuous improvement.It is part of addressing the challenge of embedding a culture of continuous learning across the Ministry. We suggest doing this by building Evaluative Thinking into our day-to-day work.

Evaluative thinking is about making a habit of using good evidence to make judgements. It is a way to approach a course of actions – a programme, briefing, even just a meeting – in a way that focusses on achieving the outcomes.

Evaluation and evaluative thinking helps us to be more intentional about our actions and to know whether or not we have got to where we wanted to go. It is more than collecting and collating data. It is a disciplined approach to inquiry and reflective practice. It helps us to use good evidence to make sound judgements.

Who are involved in this project?

This is a cross-Ministry work involving the Evidence, Data and Knowledge (EDK), Education Systems Policy (ESP), Early Learning and Student Achievement (ELSA), Systems Enablement and Support (SE&S) with representatives from the national and regional offices, the Communications team and the Web Services team. We also have a school representative from Kapiti College involved all throughout the project.

The project is helped by the coaching and mentoring of Creative HQ especially with applying a user-centric design and agile approach to developing a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) that we hope will address the problem we have set to address.

Why are we doing this? How did we do it?

In June 2018, The Ministry of Education Leadership Team has approved its Ministry-wide Evaluation Strategy Download Evaluation Strategy [PDF 1MB] that sets the direction for taking a coherent, evidence-based approach to the advice we give and the services we provide.  The strategy aims to embed continuous improvement and systematic learning into the culture across all business groups in the Ministry.

 

 

 

One of the pillars of the Evaluation Strategy is building evaluation capability, both in the Ministry and in the Sector. We approached this by using modern methods such as lean/agile working, and user-focused design. We brought together around 30 people from across the Ministry – both central and the regions - along with teachers for an intensive boot camp in how to work in this way and identify the problem definition and the elements of a solution.

When the team interviewed people from across the education sector – from teachers, to Ministry professionals to education experts – they said they want to:

  • create a culture where evaluative thinking and evaluation is valued and prioritised;
  • embed evaluative thinking and evaluation in our core business;
  • continuously ask whether we are making a difference; and
  • evaluate quickly and act early and have the courage to stop doing something if it isn’t working.

The whole solution is made up of many parts that provide the optimal conditions for change. These include:

  • A shared vision and purpose for the work and change we are engaged in;
  • An organisational culture underpinned by transparency, openness and trust;
  • Strong leadership that works across teams to collectively achieve strategic priorities;
  • A culture of trust that works collaboratively to achieve common and clearly understood goals; and
  • Clear protocols and ways of working for planning and implementation.

After identifying these, the group chose to focus on ‘sorting ourselves out first’ before we turned our attention to the wider sector. In particular, they chose to focus on building evaluative thinking in the Ministry. This was identified as being not only an important capability for the Ministry in itself, but an important precursor to growing the evaluation system. Our theory of action is that if we improve our capabilities and practices in evaluative thinking, then we will be better able to understand the impact/effect of our work and its relationship to improving student outcomes, and we will build a culture of continuous improvement.

What’s next?

We also are in the planning stage of work to build evaluative capability in the sector. This is an area of work where there is interest across the Ministry. The first phase is the ideation phase. We already have a lot of content for this through work by a cross-Ministry group at the end of last year. We will bring this together to identify the first piece of work where we can build a team to produce a tangible MVP (minimum viable product) solution.
